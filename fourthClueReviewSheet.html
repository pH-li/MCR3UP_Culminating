Arithmetic Sequences

Finally, we've moved on from trigonometry! Wasn't that a pain? Anyways, there are a couple of important terms to review:

  • Sequence: An ordered list of numbers (ie. 2, 4, 6, 8)
  • Term: Any numeric value in a sequence. They can also be identified by their position in a sequence (ie. in the sequence 2, 4, 6, 8: t3 refers to term 3, which has a value of 6)
  • Arithmetic Sequences: A sequence that has a common difference between any pair of consecutive terms (ie. the common difference is 2 in the sequence 2, 4, 6, 8). These grow linearly.
  • Note: Common differences can be found by calculating tn - tn - 1, where n can be any number in the sequence other than 1.
  • Recursive Sequence: A sequence for which 1 or 2 initial terms are given and the successive terms' values depend on the initial terms (ie. if t3 is equivalent to t2+ t1)
  • General Term: A formula with the form of tn = function, where n refers to any term in a sequence and function refers to how each term is expressed as a function of its position. For instance, if a general term is tn = 5n, and you want to find the value of the 4th term, then you'd write t4 = 5(4) = 20.
  • All arithmetic sequences have a general term which can be defined by tn = a + (n - 1)d, where a is the first term and d is the common difference value and n∈N.

    • Geometric Sequences

    Geometric sequences are very similar to arithmetic sequences, except these grow exponentially. Terms in a sequence are linked together by a common ratio instead. For instance, a successive term may be achieved by multiplying the previous value by 6.

    The common ratio can be found by calculating tn / tn - 1, where n can be any number in the sequence other than 1.

    The general term of a geometric sequence can be defined by tn = arn - 1, where a refers to the first term and r refers to the common ratio value.

    Recursive Sequences

    For the purpose of this review, there are 2 main recursive sequences to review:

  • Fibonacci Sequence
  • Defined by the recursive formula t1 = 1, t2 = 1, tn = tn - 1 + tn - 2, where n∈N and n > 2
  • Lucas Sequence
  • Defined by the recursive formula t1 = 1, t2 = 3, tn = tn - 1 + tn - 2, where n∈N and n > 2

    Financial Math

    Simple Interest

    Here's some important definitions:

  • Principal: Represented by the letter P. Refers to the initial sum of money that is borrowed or invested
  • Interest: Represented by the letter i. Refers to the money earned from an investment or the cost of borrowing money
  • Amount: The total value of an investment or loan. Is equivalent to the sum of principal and interest

    • Great! Now it's time to introduce simple interest, or money that grows linearly. With simple interest, the interest amount is only calculated on the principal.

    The formula for calculating total amount on a value being charged simple interest is F = P(1 + in), where F represents final amount, P represents principal amount, i represents interest rate, and n represents amount of time elapsed (ie. 4 months).

    Note that this formula should only be used when you are NOT paying back or withdrawing money from the bank throughout the time elapsed.

    Compound Interest: Future Value

    With compound interest, the interest is paid differently from simple interest in that the compound interest is calculated by applying the interest rate to the principal AND any interest already earned. In other words, money will grow exponentially instead of linearly.

    The formula for calculating total amount on a value being charged compound interest is F = P(1 + i)n where F represents final amount, P represents principal amount, i represents interest rate, and n represents the number of compounding periods.

    Compound Interest: Present Value

    Let's say you want to have a certain amount of money in the future, and you need to figure out how much to invest RIGHT NOW.

    Use the formula PV = A(1 + i)-n where PV represents present value, A is the total amount/future value (sometimes this is considered the amount you wish to earn in the future), i represents the interest rate per compounding period and n represents the number of compounding periods.

    Note that this formula should only be used when you are NOT investing or withdrawing money from the bank throughout the time elapsed. Additionally, anything that mentions "present value" refers to finding the value of a lump sum of money in the present moment (like... in this present moment).
